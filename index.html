Abstract

This article will dive deeper into the controversy regarding predictive policing and weigh up the advantages of predictive policing with the negatives. Predictive policing may have numerous clear advantages however there are concerns and controversy that makes the community question the need for predictive policing and these will all be visited within this article. This topic is a crucial one to cover as it will encourage open-mindedness showing both sides of the controversy and hopefully lead to improvements regarding the current Predictive Policing system. There are gaps in research about predictive modelling when it comes to clear and sufficient evidence base to prove the benefits or drawbacks.

Introduction

“Predictive policing models rely on computer systems' ability to analyse large sets of data, as well as historical crime data, to help make more effective choices and decisions about things like the location and quantity of police deployment or the identification of individuals who are likely to be victims or perpetrators of crime (Brennan Center for Justice, 2020). There are twoapplications of predictive modelling with the key difference being the input information they use to produce the predictions. The first application takes in the use of arrest data to help predict likely physical crime hotspots. The second application uses social media data to identify individuals and how likely it is that they will commit crime.”

Analysis and discussion

ADVANTAGES

1. Reduce Crime:

Predictive Policing has been said to be suitable choice of action for the reduction of crime with a few examples being able to back the claim. For instance, the police department in Dubai were able to see a reduction to major crimes by 25% within months of when they began using crime prediction (Ignesa, 2023). Departments were able to label and identify time frames and areas with higher probabilities of major crimes such as vehicle theft and burglary. This enabled them to properly allocate resources and attention to those areas which as a result led to a drop in crime overall in comparison to the previous year (Ignesa, 2023).

2. Improved Decision Making:

The use of predictive models refines and enhances decision making allowing all levels of an organisation to get insights relevant to them by their historical data (Liberties, 2025). The availability of the information in abundance discourages police from making decisions that may be based on bias rather than evidence. These predictive models are used to boost the operational efficiency of organisations and police departments (Liberties, 2025). The more efficient police departments are, the more the public benefits as order is better maintained. This is all a given if the historical data is reliable and not biased.

DISADVANTAGES

1. Privacy concerns:

With the consideration of the second application of predictive policing mentioned earlier, we can understand that it poses serious privacy concerns as it utilizes the public data about individuals gathered from the internet to predict likelihood that an individual will commit a felony. The data that is collected and stored based on anonymised arrest data likely doesn’t pose privacy risks and concerns. However, the public mining through available information and CCTV footage does pose serious risks to privacy (Liberties, 2025). Additionally, all data stored by the police department is susceptible to data leaks as data security requires training and personnel which could be quite costly. This allows for the possibility of private personal data being leaked which only adds to concerns about privacy.

2. Discrimination and Lack of transparency:

Another key concern regarding predictive modelling is that AI models can develop biases from the data that they will be taking in. This in turn can lead to policing practices that are discriminatory. Additionally predictive policing algorithms do not allow for public input on decisions regarding policing and resource allocations are made (Brennan Center for Justice, 2020). The nature of predictive policy additionally doesn’t allow the public to understand how decisions on policing are made. The public's lack of understanding as to how predictive policing is operated will naturally cause worry and raise questions when certain descions are made by the department through the use of predictive models. This in turn erodes the trust of the specific demographics and Police departments.

Conclusion

In conclusion predictive has the potential to be a great ally against crime but unfortunately reinforces racial and socioeconomic biases. However, if these factors are accounted for during the implementation of predictive policing, then it could prove a useful tool in crime reduction. For predictive policing to prove more beneficial than harmful it would intesive auditing (both publicly and independently) to provide fairness and understanding regarding the process, use other data rather than just historical data as a sole use of historical data is essentially using biased data. Lastly it would be essentially it would be essentially to have some oversight from the community, allowing members of the community to be involved in the decision-making process which would build trust and accountability. All in all, predictive policing has potential to aid in the reduction of crime but still has a few flaws that need to be accounted for before full implementation.
